A Bike Thing
& the Art of "No Exceptions"

How a UK retailer caused a customs failure with an undeclared candy — then refused to refund a customer who never received their product.

£226.04 paid.  ·  Product never received.  ·  Refund refused for 40+ days.  ·  Reason: "No exceptions."
Why this page exists

This is not a review. It is a documented record of how A Bike Thing (abikething.com) handled — and continues to handle — a situation entirely caused by their own error.

In February 2026, I ordered two motorcycle accessories from A Bike Thing, a UK-based retailer. They shipped my order to Mexico, but included an undeclared food item (a candy) inside the package — an item not listed on the commercial invoice. Mexican customs rejected the shipment entirely because of this undeclared item. The package was never delivered to me.

From the moment I reported this, A Bike Thing's response has been the same, repeated phrase: "No exceptions." Not an apology that was backed by action. Not a refund. Not a reship. Just a policy, applied rigidly to a problem they created.

Over 40 days later, I have not received my money back. Every email exchange is documented on this page, in chronological order, for anyone to read.

What Happened, Step by Step

Every date below corresponds to documented email communication, available in full in the next section.

February 17, 2026
Order placed
Purchase made — £226.04 charged to American Express
Order #144152 placed on abikething.com for a HEX ezCAN II Controller (£161.49) and a HEX ezCAN Harness Extension Kit (£45.12), plus £19.43 DHL Express Worldwide shipping. Payment processed via credit card. Order shipped same day via DHL, tracking number 1209862780.
February 24, 2026
Customs refusal — A Bike Thing's fault
DHL contacts me: package rejected at Mexican customs
Mexican customs informed DHL that the package could not be cleared because it contained an undeclared food item — a candy — not listed on the commercial invoice. This is a well-known customs regulation. A Bike Thing chose to include this item without declaring it. DHL provided photographs of the candy as evidence.

I immediately emailed A Bike Thing with the DHL photos attached, explained the situation, and asked a simple question: could they reship without the candy while the original package makes its way back?
February 25, 2026
First "no exceptions"
A Bike Thing admits the candy — and refuses to help
Gary from A Bike Thing replied acknowledging that they routinely add candy to shipments to Mexico, and that none had previously been refused. Despite admitting their practice caused the problem, he refused to reship and stated they would wait for the original package to return.

Direct quote: "We cannot send you another package, and we'll have to wait until the original one has been returned, no exceptions I'm afraid."

No apology for the delay beyond a brief acknowledgment. No offer of any remedy. A policy applied to a problem of their own making.
March 24, 2026
Follow-up — 28 days later
Nearly a month passes — still no refund on American Express
Almost a full month after the customs rejection, I followed up asking whether the refund had been issued on their end. The amount had not appeared on my American Express statement. This was a follow-up on a refund that should have been issued the moment it was clear the package was not going to be delivered.
March 25, 2026
Second "no exceptions"
A Bike Thing: "We haven't received the package back yet"
Jordan from A Bike Thing responded: the returned package hadn't arrived at their warehouse, so no refund would be issued. They escalated the matter with DHL internally.

My response was direct: "I cannot be held hostage with my payment when I never received the package. If it was held at customs or lost on its way back, I cannot be responsible for this."

I asked for an immediate refund while the DHL issue was resolved. The response was more inflexibility — and another request for the DHL contact details that had already been provided on February 24.
March 27, 2026
Ongoing refusal
Jordan repeats the same position — asks for info already provided
A Bike Thing once again declined to refund, and asked me to forward the email address or phone number from the DHL correspondence — information I had already attached in my very first email on February 24. After 31 days, they still had not reviewed the original evidence submitted.
April 5, 2026
Escalation
Chargeback filed with American Express. Final Notice sent.
Having exhausted all reasonable attempts to resolve this directly, I filed a formal chargeback dispute with American Express for the full amount of £226.04, with all documentation submitted as evidence.

A formal Final Notice email was simultaneously sent to A Bike Thing, giving them 72 hours to confirm a refund or face public documentation of this experience and escalation to UK Trading Standards and Citizens Advice.

A Bike Thing's support system confirmed receipt of the Final Notice at 8:27 PM the same day.

Assessment
The core problem with A Bike Thing's response

A Bike Thing added an undeclared food item to an international shipment bound for Mexico — a country with well-documented customs rules about undeclared goods. This was not an act of malice, but it was an act of negligence, and its direct consequence was that I never received £226.04 worth of merchandise.

What is striking is not just what happened, but how they responded before I even asked for a refund. The very first request — a simple ask to reship while the original package returned — was met immediately with "no exceptions." No assessment. No consideration. No customer service. A policy applied to a crisis they caused.

Forty days later, the same inflexibility remains. The refund is withheld until the package physically arrives back at their warehouse — a package that is lost somewhere in DHL's return chain because of their own customs error. The customer is being made to bear the financial consequence of the retailer's mistake.

Any prospective buyer of A Bike Thing should be aware: if something goes wrong with your international order — even if it goes wrong because of their error — you may find yourself facing the same two words: "No exceptions."
