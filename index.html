Why this page exists

Update — April 21, 2026 · Resolved

The £226.04 was recovered on April 21, 2026 — 63 days after the order was placed — through an American Express chargeback dispute, not through any refund action taken by A Bike Thing.

This page remains published because the experience itself is relevant to anyone considering an international order from A Bike Thing. It is not suggested that A Bike Thing would never have refunded this amount — but based on their conduct throughout this dispute, any refund would have been issued entirely on their own terms and their own timeline, regardless of fault. Anyone facing a similar issue should be aware that A Bike Thing makes no exceptions, and that initiating a claim with your credit card provider may be the most reliable path to resolution.

This is not a review. It is a documented record of how A Bike Thing (abikething.com) handled a situation entirely caused by their own error.

In February 2026, I ordered two motorcycle accessories from A Bike Thing, a UK-based retailer. They shipped my order to Mexico, but included an undeclared food item (a candy) inside the package — an item not listed on the commercial invoice. Mexican customs rejected the shipment entirely because of this undeclared item. The package was never delivered to me.

From the moment I reported this, A Bike Thing's response was the same repeated phrase: "No exceptions." Not an apology backed by action. Not a refund. Not a reship. Just a policy, applied rigidly to a problem they created. Every email exchange is documented on this page, in chronological order, for anyone to read.

American Express resolved the chargeback dispute in my favour and credited the full amount of £226.04. The funds were recovered 63 days after the original order was placed on February 17, 2026.

Assessment

The core problem with A Bike Thing's response

A Bike Thing added an undeclared food item to an international shipment bound for Mexico — a country with well-documented customs rules about undeclared goods. This was not an act of malice, but it was an act of negligence, and its direct consequence was that I never received £226.04 worth of merchandise.

What is striking is not just what happened, but how they responded before I even asked for a refund. The very first request — a simple ask to reship while the original package returned — was met immediately with "no exceptions." No assessment. No consideration. No customer service. A policy applied to a crisis they caused.

Over the following weeks, the same inflexibility continued — eventually combined with a factually incorrect claim that a PayPal chargeback was blocking the refund, when the order was paid by Credit/Debit Card and no PayPal transaction ever existed.

The funds were ultimately recovered on April 21, 2026 — 63 days after purchase — through an American Express chargeback dispute, not through any refund action taken by A Bike Thing. To be fair, it is not claimed here that A Bike Thing would never have refunded the amount. It is possible they would have done so once the Amex dispute ran its course. But based on their conduct throughout, any refund would have come entirely on their own terms and timeline, with no accommodation for the fact that the problem was entirely of their own making.

Anyone ordering internationally from A Bike Thing should understand this clearly: if something goes wrong — even if it is entirely their fault — you will be told there are no exceptions. You will wait on their schedule, with no guaranteed timeline. The most effective protection is to pay by credit card and be prepared to use your card issuer's dispute process if needed. In this case, that was the decisive step, and it worked.
